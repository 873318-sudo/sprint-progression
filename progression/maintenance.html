5.0: MAINTENANCE

5.1: Endurance in a sprint?

Just like you’ve heard in the drive phase.


Patience in a 100m sprint sounded weird at first.


That is also true for endurance in the 100m.


But once again, not in the way that you might expect.


As mentioned in top speed, as soon as you hit it, it’s all about maintaining that top speed for as long as possible.


No matter how fast you are, you will decelerate near the end of the race after you hit top speed.


Don’t believe me?


Look at Usain Bolt’s splits after 70 meters⁽²⁴⁾.


60 - 70m: 0.81 (fastest) 70 - 80m: 0.82 80 - 90m: 0.83 90 - 100m: 0.83


He slowed down after he hit his top speed.


The person who looks like they are speeding up at the end of the race to win is actually the one that decelerates the least.


Once you hit the peak of the mountain, the only place you can go is down.


You have to try and stay at the top of the mountain for as long as possible.


So what does this mean?


It means that relaxation after reaching your top speed in a race becomes more critical than any other point.


Your top speed mechanics shouldn’t change.


You’re still trying to apply as much vertical force as possible in the littlest time.


If you tense up when you’re tired, what does that do?


It makes your body move slower.


Try to throw a punch when your arm is flexed vs. when it is relaxed.


Huge difference.


5.2: Importance

I believe that at this point in the website you’ve built enough knowledge to try and make a primary goal for this phase of the race.


If you thought something like:


“Be the last one to crumble”


You would be correct.


We’ve already established that the person who wins is usually the person who decelerates the least.


But…why is deceleration inevitable?


Well, it’s simple, unbelievably simple.


During anaerobic exercise (like sprinting), your body uses glycogen (like sugar) that is stored in the muscles.


This is because your body needs to produce max effort without the need of oxygen⁽²⁵⁾.


And it can only do this for around 5 to 10 seconds for maximum effort before needing oxygen.


Even more simply put: your body can’t sustain true 100% top speed for a full 100m race, no matter how good you are.


5.3: Analysis...?

Is there even an analysis for this kind of phase?


Nope, that’s why I left it out.


As we already know, the mechanics during top speed maintenance stay the same.


If your speed endurance is bad, the stuff you may notice are your mechanics falling apart. Which is not what you want.


That’s the only thing to look out for.


5.4: Application

Now the best part is this: applying speed endurance into your training, not the long-form endurance.


As speed endurance is different, it focuses on relaxation and holding your mechanics well this late into a sprint.


And you do that by sub-max sprinting.


It’s exactly as it sounds:


You do longer sprints (usually around 120-150 meters) at a sustained high effort (like 95%) with a focus on floating and not letting your mechanics fall apart.


That's. It.


And one thing to note before we end this off:


Relaxation is a skill, not a natural talent or something you “command” to happen on the day of your race.


You gain this skill from what we just mentioned:


focus on floating or “cruising” when you hit upright sprinting, don’t tense unnecessary muscles.


Now you should read the conclusion.
